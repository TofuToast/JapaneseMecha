Japanese Mecha

by Kai Chung

What first comes to mind when I say “mecha?” Do you think of the movie Pacific Rim; where pilots control twenty-story tall Jaeger robots to fight building-sized Kaiju monsters, causing earthquakes with every step? Perhaps you watched Power Rangers as a kid and dreamed of piloting giant, brightly colored mechs to fight against alien villains. Still remember the franchise Transformers, where giant humanoid robots transform into vehicles? These examples are what most people in the Western world typically envision when they think of mecha. Notice my emphasis on “Western world” because Pacific Rim, Transformers, and Power Rangers were created in part to pay homage to the sub-genre of Science Fiction that they were inspired by: Japanese Mecha. This sub-genre offers so much more than Western mecha’s fixation on the simple giant-robot-fight-monster story. In fact, Japanese mecha embodies what makes SF great: examining our reality.

Japan derived the name “mecha” from the word “mechanism” and used it to describe any machinery. For the purpose of this explication, I will be talking about the humanoid form of robots that can be piloted by humans.

With that out of the way, I want to explicate on what mecha is used to symbolize in Japanese media. Throughout my time consuming Japanese mecha media, I noticed Japanese artists have used mecha to manifest the desire of the pilot’s psyche. In other words, pilots use mechas as a tool of change to fulfill their inner desires.

WWII:

Throughout each era of this sub-genre, all Japanese mecha stories have war and conflict-related aspects. The first instance of mecha in Japanese media was in a 1943 WWII propaganda poster called “The Science Warrior Appears in New York” by artist Ryūichi Yokoyama. It depicts a gray robot, the size of a mountain, destroying NYC. This was made a year in 1942, after the height of WWI. We see that the pilot of this mecha isn’t one person, but 1943 Japan as a whole. Perhaps the desire of the “pilot” was to still be more powerful than the US. Artist Yokoyama used the “Science Warrior” as a mecha that has supreme power to destroy, granting the country the power to fulfill their desire. I believe he specifically drew a mecha to signify that the great Japanese powerhouse was manmade; Japan became great with its own ability.

Cold War:

The use of mecha in Japanese media greatly changed after 1945, when Japan surrendered to the Allies powers, and the Japanese empire was abolished. No longer was Japan a superpower, like the Nazi regime, with control over China, Korea, the Philippines, Thailand, and Malaysia. Japanese Cities were decimated by firebombs and nuclear warheads by the Allied forces, with over 200,00 deaths. Japan was no longer fixated on being a superpower of destruction and entered a new era that wished for peace and recovering. During this era, the role of mecha took a turn to become machines used to advocate for peace. Make no mistake, artists still created mechas as mankind’s most powerful and destructive machines, but the mecha were not necessarily used for that purpose. Such Japanese mecha shows (like Astro Boy, Tetsujin 28 Go, and Super Sentai) increased as Japanese households with television rose during the 1960’s economic boom. Ultimately, the focus of these shows during this show was to sell mecha toys and merchandise. Therefore, many of the mecha designs were simple and cartoonish for ease of manufacturing into toys. Significantly influenced by the developments and use of nuclear warheads, the recurring plot point was the refusal to use mechas to destroy. Instead, mechas were used to fight against evil monsters and clear villains who are a threat to world peace. The popularity of these shows eventually inspired Western Mecha that many have watched during childhood. Many kids probably learned the virtues of peace and justice through these shows.

Post-Cold War:

Japanese mecha after the Cold War still explored mechas of mass destruction. But the most influential mecha shows of this era rejected the cartoonish designs and clear black and white morality from the previous era. Mecha shows in this era steered towards being more realistic. Shows who made viewers believe mecha can feasibly be built in real life. Shows who explored the gray area of morality found in war and conflict. Again, with the merchandise, this era was sparked in part after plastic manufacturing advancements enabled more detailed toys to be manufactured. The most influential show was the 1995 show Neon Genesis Evangelion by Hideaki Anno that steered this SF genre to focus on the psyche of the pilot. In this show, the intentions of the pilot are often not the wisest. I got the lesson that not everyone should be able to pilot something that can destroy the entire world at all. The main antagonist decides to use this robot to force everyone to become united, even if it meant destroying the entire world in the process. Director Anno critics how mecha are used with big stick ideology in mind in Japanese Mecha shows in the previous era. Is forcibly created peace and unity justified?

Reflection

This sub-genre of SF has always presented mecha as the solution to make a change in the world. The solution being a machine that is so powerful, the world must obey the pilot’s desire. Japanese mecha was always influenced by the use of nuclear arms in reality. Where diplomacy and negotiations ultimately are decided by who has a larger, stronger nuclear arsenal. Just as nuclear warheads are manmade creations that can destroy Earth, so are the mecha created by Japanese artists. While nuclear bombs forced peace in our reality, Japanese mecha points out how they are just tools. And tools have no morals. Why should there be something so powerful that it can bring peace, but also destroy the world if the wrong person used it?
