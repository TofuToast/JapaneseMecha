Japanese Mecha

by Kai Chung

What first comes to mind when I say “mecha?” Do you think of the movie Pacific Rim; where pilots control twenty-story tall Jaeger robots their way to fight building sized Kaiju monsters, causing earthquakes with every step? Perhaps you remember watching Power Rangers as a kid and dreamed of piloting giant brightly colored mechs to fight against alien villains. Still remember the franchise Transformers where giant humanoid robots transform into vehicles? These examples are what most typical people in the Western world envision about mecha because they are the most popular. Notice my emphasis on “Western world” because Pacific Rim, Transformers, and Power Rangers were created in part to pay homage to the sub-genre of Science Fiction that they were inspired by: Japanese Mecha. This sub-genre offers so much more than Western mecha’s fixation on giant-robot-fight-monster plot. In fact, Japanese mecha embodies what makes SF great: examining our reality. History of Japanese mecha: Japan derived the name “mecha” from the word “mechanism” and used it to describe any machinery. For the purpose of this explication, I will be talking about the humanoid form of robots that can be piloted by humans. With that out of the way, I want to explicate on what mecha is used to symbolize in Japanese media. Throughout my time consuming Japanese mecha, I noticed that Japanese artists have always used mecha to manifest the desire of the pilot’s psyche. In other words, pilots use mechas as a tool of change to fulfill their inner desires. The first instance of mecha in Japanese media was in a 1943 WWII propaganda poster called “The Science Warrior Appears in New York” by artist by Ryūichi Yokoyama. It depicts a gray robot the size of a mountain that is destroying NY. Cold war era Modern era Manmade solution. Absolute power.
