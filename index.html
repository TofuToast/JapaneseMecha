Introduction: Japanese Mecha

by Kai Chung

What first comes to mind when I say “mecha?” Do you think of the movie Pacific Rim; where pilots control twenty-story tall Jaeger robots to fight building-sized Kaiju monsters, causing earthquakes with every step? Perhaps you watched Power Rangers as a kid and dreamed of piloting giant, brightly colored mechs to fight against alien villains. Still remember the franchise Transformers, where giant humanoid robots transform into vehicles? These examples are what most people in the Western world typically envision when they think of mecha. Notice my emphasis on “Western world” because Pacific Rim, Transformers, and Power Rangers were created in part to pay homage to the sub-genre of Science Fiction that they were inspired by: Japanese Mecha. This sub-genre offers so much more than Western mecha’s fixation on the simple giant-robot-fight-monster story. In fact, Japanese mecha embodies what makes SF great: examining our reality.

Japan derived the name “mecha” from the word “mechanism” and used it to describe any machinery. For the purpose of this explication, I will be talking about the humanoid form of robots that can be piloted by humans.

With that out of the way, I want to explicate on what mecha is used to symbolize in Japanese media. Throughout my time consuming Japanese mecha media, I noticed Japanese artists have used mecha to manifest the desire of the pilot’s psyche. In other words, pilots use mechas as a tool of change to fulfill their inner desires.

WWII:

Throughout each era of this sub-genre, all Japanese mecha stories have war and conflict-related aspects. The first instance of mecha in Japanese media was in a 1943 WWII propaganda poster called “The Science Warrior Appears in New York” by artist Ryūichi Yokoyama. It depicts a gray robot, the size of a mountain, destroying NYC. This was made a year in 1942, after the height of WWI. We see that the pilot of this mecha isn’t one person, but 1943 Japan as a whole. Perhaps the desire of the “pilot” was to still be more powerful than the US. Artist Yokoyama used the “Science Warrior” as a mecha that has supreme power to destroy, granting the country the power to fulfill their desire. I believe he specifically drew a mecha to signify that the great Japanese powerhouse was manmade; Japan became great with its own ability.

Cold War:

The use of mecha in Japanese media greatly changed after 1945, when Japan surrendered to the Allies powers, and the Japanese empire was abolished. No longer was Japan a superpower, like the Nazi regime, with control over China, Korea, the Philippines, Thailand, and Malaysia. Japanese Cities were decimated by firebombs and nuclear warheads by the Allied forces, with over 200,00 deaths. Japan was no longer fixated on being a superpower of destruction and entered a new era that wished for peace and recovering. During this era, the role of mecha took a turn to become machines used to advocate for peace. Make no mistake, artists still created mechas as mankind’s most powerful and destructive machines, but the mecha were not necessarily used for that purpose. Such Japanese mecha shows (like Astro Boy, Tetsujin 28 Go, and Super Sentai) increased as Japanese households with television rose during the 1960’s economic boom. Ultimately, the focus of these shows during this show was to sell mecha toys and merchandise. Therefore, many of the mecha designs were simple and cartoonish for ease of manufacturing into toys. Significantly influenced by the developments and use of nuclear warheads, the recurring plot point was the refusal to use mechas to destroy. Instead, mechas were used to fight against evil monsters and clear villains who are a threat to world peace. The popularity of these shows eventually inspired Western Mecha that many have watched during childhood. Many kids probably learned the virtues of peace and justice through these shows.

Post-Cold War:

Japanese mecha after the Cold War still explored mechas of mass destruction. But the most influential mecha shows of this era rejected the cartoonish designs and clear black and white morality from the previous era. Mecha shows in this era steered towards being more realistic. Shows who made viewers believe mecha can feasibly be built in real life. Shows who explored the gray area of morality found in war and conflict. Again, with the merchandise, this era was sparked in part after plastic manufacturing advancements enabled more detailed toys to be manufactured. The most influential show was the 1995 show Neon Genesis Evangelion by Hideaki Anno that steered this SF genre to focus on the psyche of the pilot. In this show, the intentions of the pilot are often not the wisest. I got the lesson that not everyone should be able to pilot something that can destroy the entire world at all. The main antagonist decides to use this robot to force everyone to become united, even if it meant destroying the entire world in the process. Director Anno critics how mecha are used with big stick ideology in mind in Japanese Mecha shows in the previous era. Is forcibly created peace and unity justified?

Reflection

This sub-genre of SF has always presented mecha as the solution to make a change in the world. The solution being a machine that is so powerful, the world must obey the pilot’s desire. Japanese mecha was always influenced by the use of nuclear arms in reality. Where diplomacy and negotiations ultimately are decided by who has a larger, stronger nuclear arsenal. Just as nuclear warheads are manmade creations that can destroy Earth, so are the mecha created by Japanese artists. While nuclear bombs forced peace in our reality, Japanese mecha points out how they are just tools. And tools have no morals. Why should there be something so powerful that it can bring peace, but also destroy the world if the wrong person used it?

Explore the Genre

  • 1963 Astro Boy by Osamu Tezuka.

    This is the earliest Japanese mecha series to have both a comic and a cartoon show, created by Osamu Tezuka. The comic series was also my first introduction to Japanese mecha; it can be read in the Seattle Public Library! The story follows the story of Astro Boy, a robot boy, who wants to live a normal child’s life but also happens to be the world’s most powerful robot. With 1,000,000 horsepower, powered by a tiny nuclear reactor, rocket thruster in his legs, and machine guns in his butt, Astro Boy can lift entire buildings into the sky. Tezuka created the best representation of Japan’s cautionary hope that nuclear energy and machines of mass destruction can be used for good. Most importantly, Astro Boy extrapolates discrimination in our world through humankind’s refusal to see robots as anything other than just slaves in the story.

  • Super Sentai by Shotaro Ishinomori.

    This is the series that created the blueprint that the American show Power Rangers followed. I wouldn’t be surprised if you thought it was the same as Power Rangers (because it really is). It was a live action series where teenagers had the power to turn into superheroes wearing bright colored spandex suits who fought against aliens with giant mechas. It isn’t hard to guess that this series was made to sell mecha toys. Most significantly, this series was one of the first to introduce the concept of mechas combining into an even larger mecha for this sub-genre. While this series doesn’t necessarily explore how powerful machines are used badly per say, it did have a major influence in shows later on, like Neon Genesis Evangelion.

  • 1979 Mobile Suit Gundam by Yoshiyuki Tomino

    This is the first TV show in the Gundam franchise by Yoshiyuki Tomino, first airing in 1979. Most significantly, Gundam steered Japanese Mecha towards being grounded in reality and focused on military use of mecha. Gundam refers to the most powerful mecha in this show that is piloted by the main character. Instead of focusing solely on awesome giant mecha battles in space, though, much of the show speaks about the horror of war and conflict. While there is a supposed terrorist rebellion group the United Nations must fight against, viewers learn that both sides are equally cruel. More often than not, it is the rebellion group that cares more about the people. Furthermore, director Tomino purposely made it so that even the main character doesn’t know why he is fighting anymore. Only one thing is clear: he must win.

  • 1993 Patlabor 2: The Movie by Mamoru Oshii.

    Released in 1993, this movie builds off the TV series Patlabor. The Patlabor franchise is set in a Japan where police use mecha against the misuse of mecha labor machines. This is one of the first Japanese Mecha anime with direct commentary on the political climate of post-Cold War Japan. Specifically, director Oshii questions the authenticity of the peace Japan has created, claiming that it is merely an illusion while the rest of the world is in conflict. Mecha is used, but not the focus of this film. Instead, it is how the government is using them to maintain order in Tokyo. I recommend reading a bit about that period in Japan because many of the key references will not be understood by the Western audience.

  • 1995 Neon Genesis Evangelion by Hideaki Anno.

    This show is the first for a lot of things. Not only was it the first to focus on the themes of mental health and human unity, but it also introduced the concept of a biological mecha, where mankind creates a giant humanoid organism that is still controlled like a machine. In Anno’s story, these half-robot, half-monster abominations, called Evangelions, were so powerful that the pilots could somehow unite the souls of people. Director Anno was very much influenced by Japan’s desire for peace and unity after WWII and wanted to explore what they exactly mean. Most significantly, he asks whether it is worth removing individuality to achieve such goals. Beware of how esoteric this show is; 30 years later, nobody has come to understand fully what is going on in this series.

  • 2013 Pacific Rim, directed by Guillermo del Toro.

    This is a Western mecha action movie, but I included it because of its mutual relationship with Japanese mecha. Set in an apocalyptic world where giant monsters decimate cities, giant mecha were created to hunt the monsters. It is very much Guillermo Del Toro’s love letter to Japanese mecha, as there are many things Pacific Rim takes inspiration from this SF sub-genre: Godzilla-inspired monsters, Super Senpai mecha cockpits, and Super Senpai-like battle scenes, etc. I would argue that Pacific Rim has also benefited Japanese Mecha. By having a bit of familiarity from Super Senpai/Power Rangers and adding Japanese mecha themes, this movie was an entry point for many Americans to explore Japanese Mecha.

  • 2015 Gundam Iron Blooded Orphans (IBO)

    This Japanese mecha show was also popular among the Western audience because it explored the reality of poverty and the oppressed. A stark contrast to Western Mecha that who is based solely on simple action. Perhaps Pacific Rim also helped with Gundam IBO’s popularity. The show was set in a world where every planet in our solar system was colonized and controlled by an empire on Earth that maintains power with giant mecha. The story of this show is a first in Japanese mecha in that it focuses on the perspective of the impoverished and oppressed people on Mars who formed a resistance to get freedom from Earth. The typical trope where the main character gets the most powerful mecha is turned around in Gundam IBO as the MC is an extremely talented pilot who can perfectly control a 300-year-old mecha.
